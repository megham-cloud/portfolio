Problem:

Quarantining during the coronavirus pandemic reminds me about how much I miss traveling and how life can be short. Why not create an efficient travel planning mobile app for today’s tourists to make the most of their time traveling?

Deliverables:

User Personas, Empathy Map, Affinity Diagram, Competitor Analysis, Storyboard, User Flow, Wireframes, Prototypes

My Role:

UX Designer

Timeline:

August - September 2020

Traveling has been on my mind shortly since I started quarantining at home, so I wanted to design a new travel app to help fellow travelers easily plan their travels after the pandemic ends. I will use the following process to design this app in four weeks (as this was my first design project, I followed this process very linearly):

First, I needed to understand what troubles and emotions travelers were having during their planning process by conducting user interviews. I also wanted to ask about what apps or tools they were using for planning, so I could get an idea of what competitor travel apps were doing right and places where they could improve.

With four weeks to complete my design, I took off to do user research.

User Research

To understand my users, I had decided to ask the following questions:

  1. Recall the last trip you had and tell me about it.
  2. Do you recall how you planned for that trip?
  3. Why did you go to specific stops on that trip?
  4. What tools or methods did you use to plan?
  5. How did you feel during the travel planning process?
  6. What was the hardest part about planning?
  7. What did you enjoy the most about planning?
  8. How did you prioritize where you planned to travel to?
  9. How effective was your plan?
  10. Is there anything you could have done to improve your planning process?

I should note that I checked if my interviewees had at least one traveling experience in the past couple of years for a better understanding of how today’s travelers planned.

From interviewing five travelers, who were all college age students in their early 20’s, I found that they had a common need of wanting to visit unique, local-recommended places in order to avoid the same three to ten popular tourist attractions that were often over-hyped on the internet. Four out of five of my travelers planned trips based on word-of-mouth from other tourists who had been to the area or by asking locals, such as Airbnb hosts, what destinations they recommended. Most of my travelers wished that they could have planned their travels to be more efficient about transportation time between tourist spots.

“We actually ended up going to a snowboarding resort thanks to our Airbnb host’s suggestion. That same Airbnb host recommended this fairly unknown underground garden, which was a really cool find.”- Andrew, 21

“The beach that we went to did not seem to live up to the reviews...I think the reviews kind of overhyped the beach. It took, like, two hours to get there.”- Nam, 22

I also surveyed 17 travelers to learn about their travel planning pains and planning tools that they used. The survey results reveal that the most frustrating pain point is paying for expensive tourist attractions. Most of the people surveyed used Google Maps, Reddit, and Yelp in order to plan their trips.

Meeting my interviewees and studying the survey results helped me envision the traveler that I was designing for. Someone who is very smart and savvy about their time...

My user persona for the Informed Traveler, Miriam Garcia

Someone like Miriam Garcia. She’s a sharp, bright master’s graduate who is planning a refreshing break in Costa Rica before pursuing a PhD. Miriam knows a tourist trap when she sees one online, and nothing annoys her more than spending a few hours searching for unique traveling experiences only to be repeatedly recommended the same ten tourist traps.

With Miriam here, I could now start to define a problem specific to her for my app to solve.

Definition

Miriam can easily plan which stops she wishes to make on her trips with the aid of search engines and reviews online, but only the most popular tourist attractions show up in the results or are recommended by other travelers, which causes Miriam great frustration. I need to give Miriam an easier way to view authentic, local-approved traveling experiences without having to dig through the internet to find them.

How might I create a travel planning app so that travelers can easily and quickly find authentic experiences that fit within their budget and vacation time?

Ideation

Now that I have formed Miriam’s problem into an actionable statement, I could begin to come up with ideas for my travel app. First, I wanted to reaffirm Miriam’s needs and what my travel app could offer to meet them.

This value proposition canvas shows how we can meet the expectations and solve the frustrations of Miriam

Around the time that I created this, I named my app The Informed Traveler as this app will serve as a one-stop shop to help travelers like Miriam find unique places to visit thanks to input from locals.

I took note of what other travel planning apps were doing well, such as the ability to see reviews from people who have been to a particular location like on Yelp or the ability to judge the quality of a comment based on the ratio of upvotes to downvotes it has as seen on Reddit. In addition, I also jotted down several features for The Informed Traveler that I thought Miriam would like, including the ability to see the safety rating of a location and the ability to sort travel destinations by personal preference.

After organizing these potential ideas based on what I thought would have the greatest impact on the user and would fit into the amount of time that I had given myself to design the app, I decided on designing a review system for travel destinations as well as a way to filter travel destinations by personal preferences.

In order to start prototyping, I translated Miriam’s journey through The Informed Traveler into a user flow. As detailed in the flow, Miriam will be able to

  • Create an account to keep track of her travel plans
  • Search and filter destinations to add to a travel plan
  • Write a review for a destination she has been to
  • View reviews from other travelers for any destination
  • View her travel plans
  • Manage her account

This first run of sketches passed user testing, so I jumped straight into creating a low-fi prototype for The Informed Traveler using Adobe XD.

Feel free to interact with this prototype!

In these frames, I wanted to include an emoji feature for showing how travelers felt towards a location. After testing my low-fi prototype on some users, I found that the emoji reaction was extraneous and that users would rather just use the upvote/downvote system for showing appreciation of a location and leave a comment if they really felt strongly about a location.

Other popular feedback included users needing the ability to distinguish the filter button for sorting traveling destinations and the ability to distinguish comments made by travelers from comments made by locals.

As I was focused only on the UX aspect of designing The Informed Traveler and making sure that design solved Miriam’s problem I had defined, I considered my design finished for now.

Reflection

My takeaway from this experience is to not get caught up in feature creep, like allowing users to add an emoji reaction to a travel spot they had been to. I feel like I have spent a lot of time thinking about all the features that I could include in the app when I had already narrowed down that list based on feedback from user interviews and user testing. While making prototypes, I kept thinking of more features to place into the app, but I needed to focus more on the features prioritized.

For the future of The Informed Traveler, I may consider working on updating the UI of the app to give it more personality and enjoyment for the users.

For future projects, I will focus more on prioritized features. I will also try to remember to iterate as early and often as I can so that I do not get too caught up in one idea for a project.
